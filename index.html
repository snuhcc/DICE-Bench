Abstract

We present DICE-BENCH, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate the tool-use capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in multi-round, multi-party dialogues—a challenging scenario that mirrors real-world group chat environments.

Our benchmark synthesizes realistic group conversations with 2-4 participants across 4 rounds, creating complex scenarios where tool-calling cues are dispersed throughout the dialogue. We introduce DICE-SCORE, a metric that quantifies dialogue difficulty by measuring the dispersion of tool-related information, with higher scores indicating more challenging inputs.

Our evaluation reveals that even state-of-the-art models like GPT-4o achieve only 64% exact match accuracy on average, with performance degrading as the number of rounds or participants increases. This highlights a significant gap in current LLMs' ability to handle complex, multi-party tool-use scenarios.

As the first benchmark to combine multi-round multi-party dialogue with inter-tool dependencies, DICE-BENCH provides a comprehensive evaluation framework with fully open code, data, and pipeline, establishing a new standard for assessing LLMs' practical tool-use capabilities in collaborative settings.

Pipeline Overview

Our data generation pipeline creates realistic multi-party dialogues with embedded tool-use requirements, ensuring diverse and challenging scenarios for LLM evaluation.

Performance Comparison

Performance comparison across different models shows consistent degradation as dialogue complexity increases, with all models struggling particularly in 4-round, 4-party scenarios.

Method Highlights

🎯 Realistic Scenarios

DICE-BENCH synthesizes authentic group chat scenarios with natural turn-taking and context-dependent tool requirements, reflecting real-world collaborative problem-solving.

📊 DICE-SCORE Metric

Our novel metric quantifies input difficulty by measuring how tool-related information is dispersed throughout the dialogue, enabling fine-grained performance analysis.

🔧 Inter-tool Dependencies

The benchmark includes complex scenarios requiring multiple tools with dependencies, testing LLMs' ability to understand and execute multi-step tool chains.
